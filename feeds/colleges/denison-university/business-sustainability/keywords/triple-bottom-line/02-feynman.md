# Triple Bottom Line — Feynman Explanation
# បាតបន្ទាត់ទាំងបី — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Imagine your uncle runs a small ice factory (រោងចក្រទឹកកក) outside Siem Reap. At the end of each month, he does one thing to decide if the month was good: he counts the money left in the cash box. More money than last month? Good month. Less money? Bad month.

That single number at the bottom of his notebook — that is the **"bottom line."** One line. One number. Money.

Now let me ask you something. What if, to make all that ice, the factory:

- ran its old diesel generator day and night, filling the neighbors' air with black smoke,
- paid the two teenage boys who haul ice almost nothing, with no rest and no safety gloves,
- and pumped so much groundwater that the well of the family next door went dry?

The cash box is full. The bottom line says "good month." But is it really?

The smoke, the underpaid boys, the dry well — none of those show up in the cash box. They are real costs, but your uncle is not paying them. **Other people are paying them for him.**

---

**This is the whole idea of the Triple Bottom Line.**

A man named John Elkington said: stop using *one* bottom line. Use *three*. He called them the **3Ps**:

- **Profit (ប្រាក់ចំណេញ)** — the money. The normal bottom line.
- **People (មនុស្ស)** — the workers, the neighbors, the community. Are they better off or worse off because of your business?
- **Planet (ភពផែនដី)** — the air, the water, the soil. Is the environment better off or worse off?

A truly good month is not just "the cash box is full." A truly good month is: **the cash box is fuller, the people are treated well, AND the planet was not damaged.** Three lines, all green.

---

**Why three lines and not one?**

Because if you only measure money, you will quietly trade away the other two to make the money number bigger. Burn cheaper dirty fuel — money up, planet down. Pay the boys less — money up, people down. You *look* successful while you are actually destroying the things your business stands on.

It is like a farmer who sells the topsoil of his own field to look rich this year. Next year, nothing grows. He counted only the cash, not the soil.

---

**Here is the twist — even Elkington became unhappy with his own idea.**

In 2018, the man who invented the Triple Bottom Line said something surprising: "I want to recall it." Like a car company recalling a faulty car.

Why? Because companies had turned it into a trick. They wrote three pretty numbers in a glossy report — "look, we measure People and Planet too!" — and then changed *nothing*. The report looked green; the factory stayed dirty. Elkington said: the three lines were never meant to be decoration. They were meant to *change what you do*. If your three numbers do not change your decisions, you are doing it wrong.

---

**The simple version:**

Do not judge your business by money alone. Ask three questions every month: *Did I make money? Did I treat people well? Did I take care of the planet?* If you can only answer "yes" to the first one, you are borrowing from the future — and someday the bill arrives.
