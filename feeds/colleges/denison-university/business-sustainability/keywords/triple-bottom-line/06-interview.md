# Triple Bottom Line — Journalist Interview
# បាតបន្ទាត់ទាំងបី — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Chanthou Meas, a sustainability accounting researcher and advisor to several Cambodian export councils.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Chanthou, the phrase "Triple Bottom Line" appears in every sustainability brochure now. In plain terms, what is it?

**Dr. Chanthou Meas:** It is a way of measuring a business by three results instead of one. The old way reports a single bottom line — profit. The Triple Bottom Line, named by John Elkington in 1994, reports three: People, Planet, and Profit. The "3Ps." The argument is simple: a business consumes financial, social, and natural resources, so it should account for all three, not just the one that comes with a price tag.

**Ratana:** Why isn't profit enough? If a company is profitable, isn't it by definition succeeding?

**Dr. Chanthou:** Profit measures only the value that flows through the market at a price. But a factory also produces things the market does not price — pollution, worker fatigue, a depleted aquifer. Economists call those externalities. A company can grow its profit precisely by pushing those costs onto others. So profit alone can rise while the company is quietly destroying the very community and environment it depends on. The Triple Bottom Line exists to make that destruction visible before it becomes a crisis.

**Ratana:** Can you give a Cambodian example?

**Dr. Chanthou:** The garment and footwear sector is the clearest. For years some factories reported strong profit while their People line — wages, working hours, factory air quality, the fainting incidents we saw during heat waves — was never on any account a manager looked at. When international buyers began demanding social audits, those unmeasured costs suddenly became financial: lost orders, remediation, monitoring fees. The companies that had been keeping a People account all along adapted easily. The ones keeping only a profit line were caught out.

**Ratana:** This idea is attributed to John Elkington. I read that he later tried to "recall" it. Is that true?

**Dr. Chanthou:** It is, and it is one of the most honest things a thinker has done. In 2018 Elkington wrote that he wanted to issue a "product recall" of the Triple Bottom Line. Not because it was wrong, but because it had been misused. He had hoped it would push companies toward deep transformation. Instead, many treated it as an accounting exercise — publish three numbers, win an award, change nothing. He felt the tool had been domesticated into a reporting ritual.

**Ratana:** So is the concept dead?

**Dr. Chanthou:** No — his recall was a challenge, not a funeral. He was saying: the three lines must drive real decisions, not decorate annual reports. If a poor Planet result never stops a project that the profit number would approve, then you do not have a triple bottom line. You have a single bottom line wearing a green costume.

**Ratana:** That sounds like greenwashing. How do you tell genuine Triple Bottom Line practice from a performance?

**Dr. Chanthou:** Look for three signs. First, are the People and Planet numbers measured by a credible, ideally independent method, or are they self-graded? Second, are they audited and comparable year to year, the way the GRI standards require, or do they change definition whenever the result looks bad? Third — and this is the real test — can you point to a specific business decision that was changed *because* of a social or environmental result. If management can name a project they redesigned or rejected for People or Planet reasons, the framework is alive. If they can only show you a glossy report, it is theater.

**Ratana:** Critics say measuring People and Planet is too vague — you can't add a polluted river to a profit number. Is that fair?

**Dr. Chanthou:** It is a real difficulty, and Elkington himself warned against forcing all three into one currency. The point of keeping three separate accounts is precisely that you *shouldn't* convert a poisoned river into dollars and bury it in the profit line, because then a good profit can mask a bad environment. Three lines, in three different units, kept honestly side by side, is messier than one number — but it is more truthful. And truth is the entire purpose of accounting.

**Ratana:** Final question. For a small Cambodian business owner — a rice miller, a furniture maker — is the Triple Bottom Line a luxury for big corporations, or something they should care about?

**Dr. Chanthou:** It is increasingly survival, not luxury. The buyers, the banks, and the export markets that Cambodian businesses sell into are all moving toward asking for the other two lines. A miller who already knows their water use and treats their workers well will be ready when the EU buyer asks. The one who has only ever counted cash will be scrambling. The Triple Bottom Line is simply the discipline of knowing the full truth of your business — and that truth is no longer optional.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Chanthou.
