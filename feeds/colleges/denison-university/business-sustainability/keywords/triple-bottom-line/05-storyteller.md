## The Brick Kiln on the Red Road / ឡឥដ្ឋនៅផ្លូវក្រហម

Sok Pheakdey inherited the brick kiln from his father, on the red laterite road south of Kampong Cham. For thirty years his father had kept the books in a single school exercise notebook: bricks sold, money in, money out, and one number circled in red pen at the bottom of each page — the profit. His father called it "the only line that tells the truth."

When Pheakdey took over, construction in the province was booming. He pushed the kiln harder than it had ever run. He bought cheaper fuel — scrap wood and, increasingly, old tires, which burned hot and cost almost nothing. He hired more workers, paying them by the brick, and the fastest families practically lived at the kiln, children stacking clay beside their parents. Each month the red-circled number at the bottom of the notebook grew. By his father's measure, Pheakdey was the most successful kiln owner the family had ever produced.

Then three things happened in the same year.

First, the rice farmers whose paddies lay downwind began to complain. The black smoke from the burning tires settled on their young rice, and yields fell. They were not Pheakdey's customers, not his workers, not his investors. They simply breathed his air and grew rice in his shadow. His notebook had no line for them.

Second, a labor inspector visited after a child stacking bricks was burned on the arm. The inspector asked to see employment records, ages, rest hours. Pheakdey had none. His notebook recorded bricks and riels — never the people who made them.

Third, his largest buyer — a Phnom Penh construction firm now building for an international hotel chain — sent a letter. The hotel's owners required all suppliers to certify their bricks were made without child labor, without toxic fuel, and without harm to surrounding farms. The buyer needed Pheakdey to document it. Pheakdey looked through thirty years of notebooks and realized he could document none of it, because he had never measured any of it.

He lost the contract. Not because his bricks were bad or his price too high — his profit line had never looked better — but because two other lines, which he had never thought to keep, turned out to be the lines his biggest customer now cared about most.

---

A young woman from a development NGO came to study the kilns of the province. She sat with Pheakdey over palm sugar and tea and asked to see his books. He showed her the single red-circled number.

"Your father measured one thing," she said. "The world now measures three." She drew three lines on a clean page. *Profit*, she wrote on the first — and pointed at his notebook: "This you have always kept." *People*, she wrote on the second — "The workers, the children, the farmers downwind." *Planet*, she wrote on the third — "The air, the soil, the fuel you burn."

"There is a name for keeping all three," she said. "The Triple Bottom Line. A man named Elkington gave it that name when I was a child. And here is the strange part — years later he tried to take the name back. He said too many companies wrote three beautiful numbers and then changed nothing. He said the three lines were never meant to decorate a report. They were meant to change what you do."

Pheakdey was quiet for a while. Then he said: "My father was right that the bottom line tells the truth. He was only wrong about how many there are."

---

The following season, Pheakdey switched back to clean wood fuel and rice husk, which cost more but cleared the smoke from the farmers' fields. He stopped hiring children, raised the per-brick rate so adult families could earn the same income in fewer hands, and kept a second notebook — one for People, one for Planet — beside his father's old book of profit.

His profit line shrank that year. He had expected that. What he had not expected was the letter, eight months later, from the same Phnom Penh buyer: the hotel chain wanted a certified supplier for its next four projects, and Pheakdey was now the only kiln on the red road that could prove all three lines were clean.

He circled three numbers at the bottom of the page that month. Only one of them was red.
