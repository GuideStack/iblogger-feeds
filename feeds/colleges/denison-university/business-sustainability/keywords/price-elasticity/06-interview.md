# Price Elasticity — Journalist Interview
# ភាពយឺតនៃថ្លៃ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Bunthoeun Sok, pricing strategist and lecturer in managerial economics at the Royal University of Law and Economics.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Bunthoeun, "price elasticity" sounds like jargon. Why should a Cambodian business owner care about it?

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** Because nearly every pricing decision they make is secretly a bet on elasticity, whether they know the word or not. Every time an owner asks "if I raise my price, will I make more money or lose customers?" — that is an elasticity question. The owners who guess well thrive; the ones who guess badly go out of business. Elasticity just gives a name and a discipline to a judgment they are already making blind.

**Ratana:** So how does an owner actually figure out whether their demand is elastic?

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** Three questions. First: do my customers have close substitutes? If a competitor sells the same thing across the street, demand is elastic — raise your price and they leave. Second: is my product a necessity or a treat? Necessities are inelastic; treats are elastic. Third: is my price a big or small part of the customer's budget? People barely react to the price of a small item but shop hard on a large one. If the answers point to "substitutes, treat, large purchase," tread very carefully on price.

**Ratana:** Is there a Cambodian example where misjudging elasticity hurt a business?

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** Plenty in the restaurant and beverage trade. A café in Phnom Penh raises coffee prices thinking customers are loyal, but there is a café every fifty meters — demand is extremely elastic, and the regulars simply walk further. Contrast that with a village that has the only fuel station for twenty kilometers. That station faces inelastic demand and can price accordingly. Same logic, opposite situations — and the difference is entirely about the substitutes available to the buyer.

**Ratana:** This program is about sustainability. Where does elasticity enter environmental policy?

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** It is the make-or-break variable for any green tax. Take a tax on plastic bags. If shoppers have an easy substitute — bring your own cloth bag — demand for plastic bags is highly elastic, and even a small tax slashes use dramatically. That is why plastic-bag charges work so well around the world. Now take a fuel tax. In the short run fuel demand is inelastic — people must still get to work — so the tax cuts use very little at first and mostly raises revenue. Give it years, though, and demand becomes elastic as people buy efficient vehicles. So whether a green tax "works" depends entirely on the elasticity, and the elasticity depends on the time horizon and on whether good substitutes exist.

**Ratana:** That short-run versus long-run gap seems to confuse a lot of policy debate.

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** It confuses almost all of it. Critics declare a fuel or carbon tax a failure after one year because consumption barely moved. But that is the *expected* short-run result — demand is inelastic when people are locked into their current cars and homes. The behavioral change comes later, as the long-run substitutes get adopted. Judging a long-run policy by its short-run elasticity is one of the most common and most expensive analytical errors in environmental economics.

**Ratana:** Can a government deliberately make demand more elastic so its taxes work better?

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** That is the sophisticated move, yes. A fuel tax bites far harder if there is a reliable bus network, safe cycling routes, and affordable electric vehicles — because then drivers actually *have* somewhere to go when the price rises. Pairing a tax (which raises the price) with investment in substitutes (which raises elasticity) is far more effective than either alone. The tax provides the push; the substitutes provide the exit. Without an exit, you just have an angry, captive population paying more.

**Ratana:** Final question. One sentence a student should remember.

**Dr. Bunthoeun Sok:** Price tells people *whether* to buy; elasticity tells you *how much they will listen* — and almost everything in pricing, taxation, and sustainability policy turns on how loudly they are listening.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Bunthoeun.
