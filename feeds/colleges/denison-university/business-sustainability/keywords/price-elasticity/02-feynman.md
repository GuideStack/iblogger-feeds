# Price Elasticity — Feynman Explanation
# ភាពយឺតនៃថ្លៃ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Let me ask you two questions about the market.

**Question one:** if the price of salt doubled tomorrow, would you buy a lot less salt?

Probably not. You use a little salt, it is cheap even doubled, and there is no good substitute. You would grumble and keep buying almost the same amount.

**Question two:** if the price of *one particular brand* of bottled water doubled tomorrow, would you buy less of it?

Almost certainly — you would just reach for a different brand sitting right next to it on the shelf.

That difference between the two answers is the whole idea of **elasticity** (ភាពយឺត). It measures: *when the price moves, how much does the buying move?*

---

Here is a way to feel it. Imagine demand is a rubber band connecting price and quantity.

- For salt, the band is **stiff** — pull on the price and the quantity barely budges. We say demand is **inelastic** (មិនយឺត). Buyers are stuck.
- For one brand of water, the band is **stretchy** — pull a little on the price and the quantity flies far. We say demand is **elastic** (យឺត). Buyers are sensitive.

That is it. Stiff band = inelastic. Stretchy band = elastic. The word comes from the same idea as a stretchy material.

---

Now, *why* are some things stiff and some stretchy? Four simple reasons:

1. **Do you have alternatives?** Many close substitutes → stretchy. One brand of water has many substitutes. Insulin has none.
2. **Do you need it?** A necessity → stiff. Rice, medicine, the morning commute — you buy them almost no matter the price.
3. **How big a part of your money is it?** A tiny purchase (salt, matches) → stiff; you do not bother reacting. A big one (a motorbike) → stretchy.
4. **How much time do you have?** Short term → stiffer. If petrol jumps today, you still must get to work. But over years, you might buy an electric scooter or move closer to work, and then the band stretches.

---

Here is the part that makes business owners' eyes light up.

Suppose you run a stall and you are thinking of raising your price 10%.

- If your customers are **stuck** (inelastic), few of them leave. You sell almost as much, but at a higher price — your **total money goes up**.
- If your customers are **sensitive** (elastic), many of them walk away. You lose so many sales that your **total money goes down**, even though each sale earns more.

So the single most useful question a seller can ask is: *are my customers stiff or stretchy?* Raise prices on the stiff ones. Never on the stretchy ones — there, you make more money by going *cheaper* and winning volume.

---

**The simple version:**

Elasticity is just "how much does buying change when the price changes." Stiff rubber band, barely changes — inelastic, and you can raise prices safely. Stretchy band, changes a lot — elastic, and raising prices chases customers away. Substitutes, necessity, budget size, and time decide which kind of band you are holding.
