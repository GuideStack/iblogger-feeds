# Price Elasticity — Socratic Dialogue
# ភាពយឺតនៃថ្លៃ — ការសន្ទនាបែប Socratic

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

**Professor:** Rithy, suppose the price of rice in Cambodia rose by 10%. Would households cut their rice consumption by 10%?

**Rithy:** No, much less. Rice is the staple. People might waste a little less, but they will not eat 10% less rice.

**Professor:** And if the price of a particular restaurant's fried noodles rose 10%?

**Rithy:** People would switch to the noodle shop next door. The drop could be large — maybe more than 10%.

**Professor:** So the same 10% price change produced a tiny response in one case and a large response in another. How would you *measure* the difference in a single number?

**Rithy:** Compare how much quantity changed to how much price changed?

**Professor:** As a ratio of what to what?

**Rithy:** Percentage change in quantity, divided by percentage change in price.

**Professor:** That is exactly the elasticity. Why percentages and not, say, kilos and riel?

**Rithy:** Because... if I used kilos, the answer would change depending on whether I measured in kilos or tonnes. Percentages cancel the units out.

**Professor:** Precisely Marshall's reason. Now, when the ratio is bigger than one, what does that tell you?

**Rithy:** Quantity changed more than price did. Buyers are very responsive. Elastic.

**Professor:** And less than one?

**Rithy:** Quantity barely moved. Inelastic — like the rice.

**Professor:** Now a practical test. You own the noodle shop. Your demand is elastic. You raise your price. What happens to your total revenue?

**Rithy:** I lose a lot of customers. Each one pays more, but there are so many fewer of them that... my total takings probably fall.

**Professor:** And the rice seller, whose demand is inelastic, raises price?

**Rithy:** Hardly anyone stops buying. So a higher price on nearly the same quantity — revenue rises.

**Professor:** So the wisdom "raise prices to earn more" is...?

**Rithy:** Only true for inelastic goods. For elastic goods it backfires.

**Professor:** Good. Now here is the question that connects this to your sustainability course. A government wants to cut petrol use by taxing it. In the first month, does petrol demand respond a lot or a little?

**Rithy:** A little. People still have to get to work tomorrow; they cannot change their motorbike or their home overnight. Inelastic.

**Professor:** And after five years of the tax staying high?

**Rithy:** More response. People buy efficient vehicles, move closer to work, take public transport if it exists. The demand becomes elastic over time.

**Professor:** So the same tax has what effect on consumption in the short run versus the long run?

**Rithy:** Short run, it mostly just raises revenue and barely cuts use. Long run, it actually cuts consumption a lot.

**Professor:** And which determinant of elasticity did you just invoke?

**Rithy:** Time horizon. More time means more substitutes available, so demand stretches.

**Professor:** So if a policymaker judged a fuel tax a failure after one year because consumption barely fell, what mistake would they be making?

**Rithy:** Confusing short-run inelastic demand for permanent inelasticity. They would be giving up right before the long-run response kicks in.

**Professor:** You now understand why elasticity is not a footnote. It decides whether an entire environmental policy works.
