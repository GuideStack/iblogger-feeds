## Two Stalls in the Same Market / ផ្សារតែមួយ តូប​ពីរ

In the wet market of Siem Reap, two women sold from stalls almost facing each other. Both were named Srey; the regulars called them Srey Salt and Srey Mango, after what each was known for.

Srey Salt sold salt, fish sauce, dried chili, and the small dull necessities that every kitchen needs and no kitchen can do without. Srey Mango sold fresh mangoes, rambutan, and whatever fruit was glamorous that week — the things people buy when they feel like a treat.

One year the supplier prices rose for both of them. Each woman had to decide whether to pass the increase on to customers. They decided differently, and the difference taught them — and a young economics student who watched them for a school project — what a textbook could not.

Srey Salt raised her prices. A bag of salt went from 1,000 to 1,200 riel. Did her customers revolt? They did not. A woman cooking for her family needs salt the way she needs water; she is not going to ride to another market to save 200 riel on a thing she buys once a fortnight. There is no real substitute for salt, and it is a tiny part of anyone's spending. Srey Salt sold almost exactly as much as before — but now each sale earned more. Her takings rose.

Srey Mango watched this and thought, *I will do the same.* She raised her mango price by the same proportion.

Disaster. Mangoes are not salt. A mango is a small luxury, and the stall across the way — and three others within a hundred meters — sold mangoes too. The moment Srey Mango's price ticked up, her customers simply drifted to the cheaper stalls, or decided they did not need mango this week at all and bought bananas instead. Her sales collapsed. Even though each mango she *did* sell earned a little more, she sold so many fewer that her total takings fell hard. Within a week she quietly dropped her price back, and lower still, to win the customers back.

The student, writing his report that evening, finally understood the word his teacher kept repeating: **elasticity**.

Srey Salt's customers were *inelastic* — stuck. No substitutes, a necessity, a trivial share of the budget. Raise the price and they barely flinch, so raising the price earns more.

Srey Mango's customers were *elastic* — sensitive. Many substitutes, a pure luxury, easy to skip. Raise the price and they vanish, so raising the price earns less.

Same market. Same supplier shock. Same percentage increase. Opposite outcomes — because the two goods sat at opposite ends of the elasticity spectrum.

The student added one more paragraph, the one his teacher praised most. He had asked Srey Mango what she would do if she were the *only* mango seller in the whole province. She laughed. "Then I would raise the price like Srey Salt and you could do nothing about it." Even glamorous, skippable mangoes become inelastic, he realized, when the buyer has nowhere else to go. Elasticity was not a fixed property of mangoes. It was a property of *choices* — and choices could be taken away.

That last insight, the teacher noted in the margin, is exactly why economists worry about monopoly, and exactly why a fuel tax cuts consumption far more after ten years than after ten days: give buyers time and alternatives, and even the stickiest demand begins to stretch.
