## The Cooperative That Stopped Doing Everything / សហករណ៍ដែលឈប់ធ្វើគ្រប់យ៉ាង

When Mealea returned from university to her home commune in Kampong Thom, she found the farming cooperative in trouble. Her grandmother had founded it: forty families who grew rice, raised chickens, wove baskets, and pressed palm sugar — all of it, all year. "We do everything ourselves," her grandmother used to say with pride. "We depend on no one."

But the books told a harder story. The cooperative was busy from dawn to dark and still barely breaking even. Mealea spread the ledgers across the wooden floor and tried to understand why.

She noticed something. The cooperative's rice was extraordinary — the soil, the seed strain her grandmother had guarded for decades, the families' skill. Their rice yields per hectare were among the highest in the district. But their baskets were ordinary, their palm sugar inconsistent, and their chickens no better than anyone's.

The families spent nearly half their labor hours on baskets, sugar, and chickens — work at which they were merely average — and only half on rice, at which they were exceptional.

---

Mealea remembered a lecture. A professor had drawn a doctor who could type faster than her receptionist, yet should never type her own letters, because every typed minute cost a patient. *The true cost of a thing,* the professor had said, *is the best thing you gave up to make it. Opportunity cost.*

She looked at the ledgers again with new eyes. Every hour a family spent weaving an ordinary basket was an hour stolen from growing extraordinary rice. The baskets weren't cheap to make — they were enormously expensive, because of the magnificent rice those hours could have produced instead.

"We are like the doctor washing her own dishes," she told the cooperative council that evening. Some of the elders frowned. Her grandmother frowned most of all.

"We have always made our own everything," her grandmother said. "Depending on others is weakness."

"Grandmother, we are not weak at rice. We are the best for kilometers. We are only *average* at baskets. Every basket we weave is rice we throw away." She laid out a plan: the cooperative would specialize in rice — almost entirely — and *trade* with the basket-weaving village to the east and the palm-sugar makers to the south, who were genuinely better at those crafts. "They give up little to make baskets. We give up a fortune. Let them weave. Let us grow rice. Then we trade, and both of us end up with more of everything."

---

It took a season to convince them. But the next year, the families shifted most of their hours to rice. They sold the surplus and bought baskets and sugar from the neighboring villages — better baskets and better sugar than they had ever made themselves, and still had money left over.

The cooperative, which had spent generations "doing everything," now did almost one thing — and was richer than it had ever been. The basket village, freed to focus on baskets, prospered too. Neither was weaker for depending on the other. Both were stronger.

This was David Ricardo's two-hundred-year-old law, lived out on a wooden floor in Kampong Thom: *trade by what you give up the least, and everyone gains.*

---

But Mealea added a chapter her professor's chalk diagram had left out.

A trader from the city offered the cooperative a tempting deal: he would buy unlimited rice if they switched to a cheap chemical fertilizer and pumped the paddies dry each dry season to squeeze in an extra crop. The price he offered would have made their rice the "cheapest" around — an even bigger comparative advantage.

Mealea did the arithmetic the old way and the new way. The old way said yes: lower cost, more sales, a sharper edge. The new way — her grandmother's actual way, she realized — counted the soil. The chemical regime would exhaust the very soil that made their rice exceptional in the first place. Their "advantage" would be borrowed from the land, and in a few years the land would foreclose.

"An advantage that eats its own foundation is not an advantage," she told the trader. "It's just a bill we haven't received yet."

She turned him down. The cooperative kept its slower, cleaner methods — and kept the soil that was the true, lasting source of everything they gave up the least to grow.

Her grandmother, listening from the doorway, finally smiled. "So," she said. "We depend on the basket village now. But never on borrowing from the ground."

"Yes, Grandmother," Mealea said. "That's the whole lesson, in two sentences."
