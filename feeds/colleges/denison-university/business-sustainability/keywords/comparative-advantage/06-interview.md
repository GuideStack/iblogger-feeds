# Comparative Advantage — Journalist Interview
# អត្ថប្រយោជន៍ប្រៀបធៀប — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Borann Hok, a trade economist and advisor to Cambodia's export-development working groups.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Borann, comparative advantage is taught in every economics class. In one sentence, what is it?

**Dr. Borann Hok:** It is the principle that two parties gain from trade when each specializes in what it gives up the least to produce — even if one party is more efficient at producing everything.

**Ratana:** That last part trips people up. If a country is better at everything, why trade at all?

**Dr. Borann:** Because being better in absolute terms is not the same as being cheaper in real terms. Imagine the most productive country in the world. Its workers' time is extremely valuable. If it uses that time sewing simple T-shirts, it sacrifices the high-value goods those same workers could have made — software, machinery, aircraft. That sacrifice is the opportunity cost. For a country like Cambodia, the opportunity cost of sewing T-shirts is low, because the alternative uses of that labor are less valuable. So Cambodia is the real low-cost producer of garments, and both countries are richer when each specializes accordingly.

**Ratana:** So the key idea is opportunity cost, not raw productivity.

**Dr. Borann:** Exactly. David Ricardo showed this in 1817 with England and Portugal making cloth and wine. Portugal was better at both, yet still profited by specializing in one and trading for the other. The logic is counterintuitive but airtight: what determines the gains from trade is the *difference* in opportunity costs, not who is absolutely better.

**Ratana:** How does this explain Cambodia's place in global commerce?

**Dr. Borann:** It is the entire explanation for our garment and footwear sector. Cambodia is not the best manufacturer in the world — not even close in absolute terms. But for decades our comparative advantage was abundant, relatively low-cost labor, which made labor-intensive assembly our lowest-opportunity-cost activity. Global brands, whose home-country labor is expensive, found it mutually beneficial to source from us. That is comparative advantage operating in real life, employing hundreds of thousands of Cambodians.

**Ratana:** But critics say this kind of advantage traps poor countries at the bottom. Is that fair?

**Dr. Borann:** It is a serious concern. There is a difference between *static* and *dynamic* comparative advantage. Static advantage says: you're cheap at assembly, so do assembly forever. The danger is staying locked at the lowest-value rung while never building the skills and industries to climb higher. The smart use of comparative advantage is dynamic — you start with what you have, but you reinvest the gains from trade into education, infrastructure, and higher-value production, so your advantage *upgrades* over time. Countries like South Korea did exactly that. The advantage is a launchpad, not a life sentence.

**Ratana:** Now the sustainability angle. Does comparative advantage account for environmental cost?

**Dr. Borann:** In its classical form, no — and this is its most important blind spot. Ricardo's model priced labor and assumed nothing else mattered. But suppose a country's low production cost comes partly because it lets factories pollute freely or underpay workers. That is not genuine efficiency. It is a real cost being shifted onto the environment and the workers — an externality — that simply isn't added to the price. The "advantage" is partly an unpaid bill.

**Ratana:** And that leads to terms like carbon leakage and race to the bottom?

**Dr. Borann:** Precisely. Carbon leakage is when production — and its emissions — relocates to the country with the weakest environmental rules. Global pollution doesn't fall; it just changes address, sometimes while the rich importing country congratulates itself on cleaner domestic figures. Race to the bottom is when countries deliberately weaken labor or environmental standards to manufacture a cheaper-looking comparative advantage and attract investment. Both turn the theory's elegance into a mechanism for offloading harm.

**Ratana:** So is comparative advantage outdated?

**Dr. Borann:** No — its logic is as valid as ever *within its assumptions*. The correct response is not to discard it but to widen the accounting. If we properly price carbon and enforce labor and environmental standards, then a country's comparative advantage reflects true efficiency, and trade genuinely raises global welfare. Mechanisms like carbon border adjustments are attempts to do exactly that — to stop polluting production from masquerading as a comparative advantage.

**Ratana:** Final question. For Cambodia, what is the practical takeaway?

**Dr. Borann:** Two things. First, embrace comparative advantage — it is the reason a small economy can prosper through trade with giants. Second, make sure our advantage is real. An edge built on cheap, exploited labor or an unprotected environment is fragile and, increasingly, unsellable, as buyers and importing countries start pricing those externalities. Our lasting advantage must come from skill, reliability, and sustainable practice — not from being the cheapest place to cut corners.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Borann.
