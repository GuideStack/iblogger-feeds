# Comparative Advantage — Feynman Explanation
# អត្ថប្រយោជន៍ប្រៀបធៀប — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Let me tell you about two neighbors in a village near Takeo.

The first, Bopha, is amazing. She is the fastest rice farmer in the village, *and* she is also the best weaver of silk scarves. At both jobs, she beats her neighbor Srey at everything.

The second, Srey, is slower. Her rice harvest is smaller than Bopha's, and her scarves are not as fine as Bopha's either.

Now here is the question that confuses almost everyone: **Since Bopha is better at BOTH things, should she just do everything herself and ignore Srey?**

Your gut says yes. Your gut is wrong. Let me show you why.

---

Bopha has only so many hours in a day. Every hour she spends weaving is an hour she is *not* growing rice. So when Bopha weaves one scarf, she gives up a lot of rice — because she is such a fantastic rice farmer, that hour of rice was worth a great deal.

Srey is a poor rice farmer. When Srey weaves one scarf, she gives up only a little rice — because her rice farming wasn't worth much anyway.

Do you see it? **The cost of making a scarf is the rice you didn't grow.** And that cost is *lower* for Srey, even though her scarves are slightly worse. For Srey, scarves are "cheap" to make. For Bopha, scarves are "expensive" to make, because her time is too valuable to waste on them.

---

So here's the smart move:

- **Bopha** should pour all her time into rice, where she is spectacularly good.
- **Srey** should focus on weaving scarves, because that's where she gives up the least.
- Then they **trade**: Bopha gives Srey rice, Srey gives Bopha scarves.

And the magic result: **the village ends up with more rice AND more scarves than if Bopha had stubbornly tried to do both herself.** Both women end up richer.

This is **comparative advantage**. You don't trade with someone because they're better than you. You trade because each of you should do the thing you give up the *least* to do. Economists call "what you give up" the **opportunity cost (តម្លៃឱកាស)**.

---

**Now zoom out to whole countries.**

This is exactly why Cambodia trades with rich, super-productive countries like the US or China. Those countries are better than Cambodia at almost everything — better factories, better technology, higher productivity. By your gut, they should make everything and trade with no one.

But their workers' time is *expensive*. For them to sew a simple T-shirt, they give up making something much more valuable — software, airplanes, machines. So sewing T-shirts is "expensive" for them.

For Cambodia, sewing T-shirts gives up very little, because the alternative uses of that labor were less valuable. So Cambodia is the low-opportunity-cost T-shirt maker. Both sides gain: Cambodia exports garments, rich countries focus on high-tech, and everyone consumes more.

Cambodia doesn't have to be the *best* at anything. It just has to be the one who gives up the *least* at something.

---

**But here's the warning the old theory left out.**

What if Cambodia's "advantage" in cheap T-shirts only exists because factories underpay workers or dump dye into the river without paying for the cleanup? Then it's not a real advantage — it's just a cost that someone *else* is secretly paying (the workers, the river, the planet). Economists call this a **race to the bottom**, and pollution that simply moves to wherever the rules are weakest is called **carbon leakage**. A true advantage makes everyone better off. A fake one just hides the bill.

---

**The simple version:**

Do what you give up the least to do, and trade for the rest — and both sides win, even when one is better at everything. Just make sure your "advantage" is real efficiency, not an unpaid bill handed to your workers or your river.
