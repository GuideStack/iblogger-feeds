# Comparative Advantage — Socratic Dialogue
# អត្ថប្រយោជន៍ប្រៀបធៀប — ការសន្ទនាបែប Socratic

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

**Professor:** Pisey, suppose a skilled doctor in Phnom Penh is also the fastest typist in her clinic — faster than any of the receptionists. Should she type her own letters?

**Pisey:** If she's the fastest, it seems efficient for her to do it.

**Professor:** She is the fastest at typing. But what does she give up during the hour she spends typing?

**Pisey:** An hour of seeing patients.

**Professor:** And an hour of seeing patients is worth — compared to an hour of a receptionist's time?

**Pisey:** Far more. The doctor's hour is extremely valuable.

**Professor:** So when the doctor types, what is the true cost of that typing?

**Pisey:** The patients she didn't see. Which is huge. The receptionist gives up much less by typing.

**Professor:** So even though the doctor is *better* at typing in absolute terms, who should actually do the typing?

**Pisey:** The receptionist — because the receptionist gives up less to do it.

**Professor:** You have just stated the law of comparative advantage. What is the name for "what you give up to do something"?

**Pisey:** Opportunity cost.

**Professor:** Good. Now notice — being absolutely better at a task did not settle who should do it. What settled it?

**Pisey:** The opportunity cost. Who sacrifices the least.

**Professor:** David Ricardo showed this in 1817 with England and Portugal. Portugal was better at making *both* wine and cloth, yet it still profited by specializing in one and trading. Does that surprise you?

**Pisey:** A little. If Portugal was better at both, I'd think it should make both and trade with no one.

**Professor:** That is the same mistake as the doctor typing her own letters. Why is it a mistake at the level of a whole country?

**Pisey:** Because Portugal's workers have limited time too. Time spent on cloth is time not spent on wine. If wine is where they give up the least, they should pour their effort there and trade for cloth — and end up with more of both.

**Professor:** Precisely. Now apply it. Cambodia trades garments to countries far more productive than itself. By the naive logic, should those rich countries make their own garments?

**Pisey:** No. Their workers' time is too valuable — they give up high-tech production to sew shirts. Cambodia gives up much less. So Cambodia is the low-opportunity-cost garment maker, and both gain from trade.

**Professor:** So Cambodia need not be the *best* at anything?

**Pisey:** Right. It only needs to give up the least at something.

**Professor:** Now let me trouble the theory. Suppose Cambodia's low cost in garments comes partly because factories pay below a living wage and discharge dye into a river untreated. Is that a genuine comparative advantage?

**Pisey:** It looks like one on paper. But the low cost is partly because someone isn't being paid — the workers, or the river. Those are real costs, just pushed onto others.

**Professor:** We have a name for costs pushed onto third parties. Do you recall it?

**Pisey:** Externalities. So the "advantage" is partly a hidden externality, not true efficiency.

**Professor:** And if the dirtiest production simply migrates to whichever country has the weakest environmental rules, what happens to global pollution?

**Pisey:** It might not fall at all — it just moves. That's carbon leakage. And countries weakening their rules to attract that production is a race to the bottom.

**Professor:** So is Ricardo wrong?

**Pisey:** No — his logic is correct *within his assumptions*. He assumed labor was the only cost and ignored pollution. The critique isn't that the math is wrong; it's that the assumptions leave out costs that sustainability forces us to count.

**Professor:** A precise distinction. So what should a country do with a comparative advantage built on low wages?

**Pisey:** Not stay stuck in it. Use the trade gains to upgrade — move toward higher-value, cleaner production over time, so the advantage becomes real efficiency rather than an unpaid bill.

**Professor:** That is the modern view exactly. Comparative advantage is not a destiny to accept, but a starting point to improve.
