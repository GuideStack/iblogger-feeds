# Supply and Demand — Feynman Explanation
# តម្រូវការ និងការផ្គត់ផ្គង់ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Imagine the morning fish market at Phsar Chas. The boats come in at dawn. Fishermen lay out their catch on ice. Buyers — restaurant cooks, market resellers, mothers shopping for lunch — walk the aisles.

Now watch what happens to the price, because nobody is in charge of it.

It is six in the morning. Lots of fresh fish, lots of eager buyers. A seller asks 12,000 riel a kilo. Someone pays it. So the next seller asks 12,000 too.

Now it is ten in the morning. The cooks already bought what they needed. Few buyers are left, but there is still fish on the ice — and fish does not keep. What does the seller do? She drops the price. 9,000. Still no buyers. 7,000. Now someone takes it, because it is cheap enough to be worth the bother.

You just watched **demand** and **supply** set a price with nobody deciding it.

---

Let me say the two rules plainly:

- **Demand rule:** the cheaper something is, the more people want to buy. Raise the price, fewer buyers. This is just common sense — at 3,000 riel everyone buys fish; at 30,000 only the fancy restaurant does.
- **Supply rule:** the higher the price, the more sellers want to sell. A high price makes it worth going out in rough weather, hiring more hands, catching more.

These two rules pull in **opposite directions**. Buyers want it cheap; sellers want it dear. Where do they meet?

They meet at the one price where the amount people want to buy exactly equals the amount sellers want to sell. We call that the **equilibrium price** (តម្លៃលំនឹង). The market quietly searches for it every single morning.

---

How does it find it? By pressure.

- If the price is **too high**, fish is left over (a **surplus** / លើស). Leftover fish forces sellers to cut the price.
- If the price is **too low**, the fish sells out instantly and people are still asking (a **shortage** / ខ្វះ). Buyers compete and push the price up.

Too high pushes down. Too low pushes up. The only price with no pushing is equilibrium. The market is like a ball rolling to the bottom of a bowl — it stops where the forces balance.

---

Now the part that trips people up: **moving along a line versus the whole line jumping.**

If the price of fish changes and *that alone* changes how much people buy — you are sliding **along** the demand line.

But suppose a doctor goes on TV and says "eat more fish, it is healthy." Now people want more fish *at every price* — even at 12,000 they want more than before. The whole demand line **jumps to the right**. That is a **shift**, not a slide. New line, new meeting point, new price.

Same with supply: a fuel-price spike makes fishing more expensive, so sellers offer less at every price — the supply line jumps left, and fish gets dearer.

---

**The simple version:**

Price is not chosen by anyone. It is the number where the buyers' "I want it cheaper" and the sellers' "I want it dearer" stop fighting. Change the price alone, you slide along a line. Change anything else — income, weather, tastes, fuel, the number of buyers — and the whole line moves, and the market hunts for a new resting price.
