## The Year the Onions Cried / ឆ្នាំដែលខ្ទឹមយំ

Chenda grew shallots on two hectares of sandy soil in Kampong Cham. She had grown them for eleven years, and she thought she understood the market. She did not understand it the way the textbooks did, but she understood it in her hands and her account book, which is sometimes deeper.

In the year the story is about, the rains had been kind and the whole province planted shallots. Everyone had heard that prices the previous season were good — 4,000 riel a kilo at the farm gate. So everyone wanted that 4,000 riel.

Chenda's neighbor planted three extra rows. The man across the road, who used to grow cassava, switched entirely to shallots. The cooperative two villages over doubled its area. Nobody coordinated this. Each farmer simply looked at last year's high price and decided, privately, to supply more.

This is the first thing the story teaches: a high price does not just sit there. It is a signal, and farmers answer it by supplying more. The supply curve had shifted far to the right — not because of anything that happened in the market that year, but because of a price that had already passed.

Harvest came. The trucks rolled in from every commune at once. The collectors at the market saw mountains of shallots and very nearly the same number of buyers as always.

The price did not hold at 4,000.

A collector offered 2,800. Chenda refused and took her sacks home. The next collector offered 2,400, because by then there were even more shallots and the first sacks were already softening. Shallots do not wait. By the third day the offer was 1,700, and Chenda, doing the arithmetic of a woman who has rent and a daughter in school, sold at 1,700 and wept a little behind the truck.

This is the second thing the story teaches: when supply floods in and demand has not grown to match, there is a **surplus**, and a surplus has only one cure — the price falls until buyers are willing to absorb the extra. The market was not cruel. It was arithmetic. Too much chasing too few buyers, and the only number that clears the market is a low one.

Across the dirt road, the cassava-turned-shallot man fared worse; he had borrowed for seed and fertilizer at the high-price dream and could not cover the loan at 1,700.

The following season the lesson reversed itself, as lessons do. Burned by the crash, most farmers planted little. The cassava man went back to cassava. The cooperative cut its area. Supply shrank hard. But people still wanted shallots — demand had not gone anywhere. Now there were few shallots and the usual buyers. A **shortage**. The collectors arrived early, competed for the thin harvest, and the price climbed to 5,200 riel.

Chenda, who had planted a careful, ordinary amount — not chasing the dream, not fleeing the fear — sold the same two hectares' worth at 5,200 and paid for her daughter's whole year of school in one morning.

Years later, a young agricultural extension officer visited and drew her two crossing lines on a page: one sloping down, one sloping up, meeting at a point. "This is supply and demand," he said. "Where they cross is the price."

Chenda looked at the diagram for a long moment. "The lines move," she said. It was not a question.

"Yes," he said, surprised. "The whole line shifts when farmers all plant more or less. Most people only learn that after it has cost them."

"I learned it," Chenda said, "the year the onions cried."
