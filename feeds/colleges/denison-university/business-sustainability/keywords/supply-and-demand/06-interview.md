# Supply and Demand — Journalist Interview
# តម្រូវការ និងការផ្គត់ផ្គង់ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, agricultural market economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, supply and demand is the first thing taught in any economics class. Why does it still matter for understanding Cambodia's economy today?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because most of the volatility ordinary Cambodians feel — in rice, pork, vegetables, fuel, even rent in Phnom Penh — is supply and demand working exactly as the model predicts. People often blame "speculators" or "the government" when a price jumps, and sometimes they are right. But far more often the price moved because the underlying supply or demand moved. If you understand the model, you can tell the difference between a normal market adjustment and an actual abuse.

**Ratana:** Give me a concrete recent example.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Pork prices. When African Swine Fever hit and farmers culled herds, the supply of pork fell sharply — the supply curve shifted left. Demand was unchanged; people still wanted pork. With less supply meeting the same demand, the only possible outcome was a higher equilibrium price. No conspiracy, just the model. The useful policy question then becomes: do you attack the price directly, which creates shortages, or do you help supply recover, which is slower but actually fixes it?

**Ratana:** You mentioned attacking the price creates shortages. Can you explain that?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** If a government caps a price below the equilibrium — say, to make pork "affordable" — then at that low price buyers want a lot and sellers want to supply little. The gap is a shortage. You see empty stalls, queues, rationing, and usually a black market where the real price reappears. Price controls do not repeal supply and demand; they just push the adjustment somewhere uglier. This is one of the most reliable findings in all of economics.

**Ratana:** Many readers confuse "demand went up" with "the price went up." Are they the same thing?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** No, and the confusion is important. A price can rise because demand shifted out — more buyers, higher incomes, a new use for the product. Or it can rise because supply shifted in — a bad harvest, higher fuel costs. The price moves the same way in both cases, but the cause and the cure are opposite. If it is a demand surge, you may want more production. If it is a supply collapse, you address the cause of the collapse. Diagnosing which curve moved is the whole job.

**Ratana:** How does this connect to sustainability, which is the theme of this program?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Directly. The market's claim to efficiency rests on one assumption: that the price reflects all the costs of producing the good. For a sand-dredging operation on the Mekong, the price of sand covers the dredger's fuel and labour — but not the riverbank erosion, the lost fish habitat, the homes that collapse downstream. So the equilibrium price is artificially low, and at a low price people demand and supply *too much* sand. The market is efficiently producing an outcome that is socially destructive. That is the bridge from this keyword to market failure and to the whole environmental-economics agenda.

**Ratana:** So supply and demand is not "wrong," it is incomplete?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Exactly the right way to put it. The mechanism is real and powerful — prices genuinely do coordinate millions of decisions no planner could. But the mechanism only steers us toward a *good* outcome when prices tell the truth about costs. When they leave out pollution or resource depletion, the same powerful mechanism steers us efficiently off a cliff. The policy task in a sustainable economy is to make prices tell the truth — through taxes, property rights, or regulation — and then let supply and demand do the rest of the work.

**Ratana:** Final question. If a young Cambodian entrepreneur learns only one thing from this model, what should it be?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That a price is information, not just a number to resent or celebrate. A high price is the market shouting "we need more of this"; a low price is "we have enough." Learn to read prices as signals, learn to ask which curve moved and why, and you will make better decisions than a competitor who only reacts to the number. The farmers who survive the boom-bust cycles are the ones who read the signal instead of chasing last year's price.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
