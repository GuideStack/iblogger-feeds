# Market Failure — Feynman Explanation
# បរាជ័យទីផ្សារ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Normally, markets are a small miracle. Nobody plans them, yet the right amount of rice, shoes, and motorbikes somehow gets made. How? Through prices. A price is a little messenger. It tells buyers "this is what it costs to make me" and tells sellers "this is how much people want me." When the messenger tells the truth, the market makes just the right amount of everything.

**Market failure is what happens when the messenger lies.**

Let me show you the four big lies.

---

**Lie number one: the hidden cost (externality).**

A factory by a river makes cheap fertilizer. Its price covers chemicals, workers, electricity. But it quietly dumps waste into the river, killing the fish that feed a hundred families downstream. Those families pay a cost — but it never appears in the fertilizer's price.

So the fertilizer *looks* cheap. People buy lots of it. The factory makes lots of it. But it is only cheap because somebody else — the fishing families — is secretly paying part of the bill.

The price lied. It said "I cost X" when the true cost to society was "X plus a dead river." So the market makes *too much* of the polluting thing. That is an externality.

---

**Lie number two: the thing nobody can sell (public good).**

Think about clean air over Phnom Penh. Everyone wants it. But how do you *sell* it? You cannot put a meter on someone's nose and charge them per breath. And if your neighbor cleans the air, you breathe it for free whether you pay or not.

So here is the problem: everyone waits for someone *else* to pay. "Why should I, when I get it free anyway?" Economists call this the **free-rider problem**. The result? Almost nobody pays, and the market makes far too *little* clean air — sometimes none at all. The market simply cannot handle things that can't be locked up and sold.

---

**Lie number three: the only seller in town (monopoly).**

In a healthy market, many sellers compete, which keeps prices honest. But if there is just *one* seller — the only fuel station for fifty kilometers — that seller can hold back supply and charge whatever it likes. It deliberately makes *less* than people want, to keep the price high. Again, the wrong amount: too little, too dear.

---

**Lie number four: one side knows a secret (information asymmetry).**

You buy a used motorbike. The seller knows it has a cracked engine block; you don't. He smiles and sells it as "perfect." Because buyers *know* sellers might be hiding things, they distrust every used bike and offer low prices — which drives the honest sellers with good bikes out of the market, leaving mostly the bad ones. The whole market shrinks and rots because of unequal knowledge.

---

So here is the key idea. **A market failure does not mean the market stopped working. It means the market worked perfectly toward the *wrong answer*** — because the price messenger was carrying a lie.

And that is exactly why governments tax pollution, provide clean air and parks themselves, break up monopolies, and force sellers to disclose information. Each policy is just an attempt to make the price tell the truth again, so the market goes back to making the right amount.

---

**The simple version:**

Markets work by trusting prices. When a price leaves something out — a hidden cost, a thing nobody can sell, a lone seller's games, a buried secret — the market confidently produces the wrong amount. That is market failure: not chaos, but efficient pursuit of a bad target.
