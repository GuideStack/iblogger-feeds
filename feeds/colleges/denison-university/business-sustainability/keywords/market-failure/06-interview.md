# Market Failure — Journalist Interview
# បរាជ័យទីផ្សារ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Soriya Keo, environmental economist at the Royal University of Phnom Penh.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Soriya, "market failure" sounds like a verdict that markets are bad. Is that what economists mean?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Not at all — and this is the most common misunderstanding. Market failure does not mean the market broke down or behaved irrationally. It means the market did exactly what it is built to do, efficiently, but arrived at an outcome that is bad for society. The failure is not in the mechanism; it is in the information the prices carry. When prices leave something out, the market confidently produces the wrong amount. Economists who love markets are precisely the people most interested in market failure, because it tells you exactly where and why a market needs help.

**Ratana:** What are the main ways prices "leave something out"?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Four classic ones. Externalities — a cost or benefit landing on someone outside the transaction, like pollution. Public goods — things like clean air that cannot be sold because no one can be excluded, so they get underprovided. Monopoly — a single seller restricting supply to raise prices. And information asymmetry — one side knowing something the other doesn't, like a lender who can't tell good borrowers from bad. All four share the same root: the price stops telling the truth about social costs and benefits.

**Ratana:** Cambodia's economy has grown fast. Where do you see these failures most clearly?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Environmental externalities are everywhere here because growth has outpaced regulation. Sand dredging on the Mekong and Tonle Sap is a vivid one — the price of construction sand never includes the riverbank erosion, the collapsed homes, the lost fish. So we dredge far too much. Brick kilns burning waste, factories discharging untreated water, smoke from land clearing — each is a negative externality where the polluter's private cost is far below the true social cost. The market rewards the cheapest producer, and the cheapest producer is often the one externalizing the most harm onto others.

**Ratana:** Why can't the affected communities simply negotiate compensation from the polluters?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Sometimes they can — that is the Coase insight, when property rights are clear and few parties are involved. But environmental harms usually involve many diffuse victims, unclear rights, and large bargaining costs. A thousand families breathing the same bad air cannot easily organize to negotiate with a factory, and even if they did, who has the legal right — the factory to pollute, or the families to clean air? In Cambodia those rights are often undefined, which is itself part of the failure. When negotiation is impractical, you need public policy.

**Ratana:** What does correcting a market failure actually look like in practice?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** The principle is always the same: make the price tell the truth. For pollution, that means a Pigouvian tax or a cap-and-trade system, so the polluter pays the social cost and naturally cuts back. For public goods like clean air or protected forests, it means public provision or protected status, funded by taxes, because the market will never supply them on its own. For monopoly, competition policy. For information gaps, mandatory disclosure — eco-labels, emissions reporting. Each instrument targets a specific failure. The art is matching the tool to the failure rather than reaching for the same blunt instrument every time.

**Ratana:** Is there a risk that government intervention creates its own failures?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Absolutely, and an honest economist must say so. We have a parallel concept — "government failure" — where a tax is set wrong, a regulation is captured by the industry it is meant to control, or enforcement simply doesn't happen. In Cambodia, weak enforcement is often the binding problem: a fine on paper that is never collected changes nobody's behavior. So the existence of a market failure is a *reason* to consider intervention, not a guarantee that intervention will help. You have to compare the imperfect market with the imperfect government and ask which gets closer to the truth.

**Ratana:** Final question. Why should a business student, not just a policymaker, care about market failure?

**Dr. Soriya Keo:** Because the businesses that thrive over the long run are the ones that see market failures coming. Today's externality is tomorrow's regulation, tomorrow's carbon tax, tomorrow's consumer boycott. A company that has been quietly externalizing pollution is sitting on a hidden liability that materializes the moment the price is corrected. The sustainable business strategy is to internalize those costs *before* you are forced to — because the direction of history, here and everywhere, is toward making prices tell the ecological truth.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Soriya.
