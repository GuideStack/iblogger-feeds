## The Cheapest Bricks in the Province / ឥដ្ឋថោកបំផុតក្នុងខេត្ត

When Pheakdey opened his brick kiln on the edge of the commune, the other kiln owners did not understand how he undercut them so badly. His bricks sold for almost a third less, and they were perfectly good bricks. Builders came from three districts away. Within a year he had the busiest kiln in the province and was building a second house in Phnom Penh.

His secret was not skill. It was fuel. The other kilns burned rice husk and wood, which cost money. Pheakdey burned old tires, scrap rubber, and whatever the recyclers in the city paid him to take away. He was paid to receive his fuel. So his cost of firing each batch of bricks was almost nothing, and he passed the saving on, and the market rewarded him for it with customer after customer.

This is the first thing to understand about the story: the market was working *perfectly*. Buyers wanted cheap bricks; Pheakdey supplied cheap bricks; the price found its level; everyone in the transaction was happy. By the textbook test of a competitive market — buyer satisfied, seller satisfied, price clearing — nothing was wrong at all.

And yet something was deeply wrong, and it was happening to people who were never in the transaction.

The smoke from burning tires is black and oily and it does not rise cleanly. It settled over the three villages downwind of the kiln. Mrs. Channary's youngest developed a cough that did not leave through two rainy seasons. The rice in the fields nearest the kiln grew a film of soot. An old man two hundred meters away, who had breathed clean commune air for seventy years, began to wheeze climbing his own stairs. None of them had bought a single brick. None of them had been asked. None of them were paid one riel.

Here is the heart of it. The true cost of Pheakdey's bricks was not low. It was enormous — it included a child's lungs, a farmer's fouled crop, an old man's breath. But none of that cost appeared in the price. The price counted only what Pheakdey paid: nearly nothing. So the price *lied*. It announced "these bricks are cheap" when the honest statement was "these bricks are cheap for the buyer and ruinous for the neighbors."

And because the price lied in that particular direction — too low — the market did exactly what a market does with anything too cheap. It made *more* of it. More orders, more batches, more tires, more smoke. The very efficiency of the market, the thing that is usually its glory, drove the harm faster. A clumsy market might have hurt the villages slowly. An efficient one hurt them at full speed.

The villagers did what the powerless often do first: they endured. Then Mrs. Channary, whose child still coughed, walked to the commune office. The commune chief, an unhurried man named Sovann, listened, then did something simple. He asked the health post to count. How many respiratory complaints in the three downwind villages this year, compared with three years ago? The number had nearly tripled.

Sovann understood, without ever having heard the phrase, what an economist would call a **negative externality**. The kiln was imposing a cost on third parties that the price did not capture, and so the market was producing far too much of the polluting good. Pheakdey was not evil; he was responding rationally to a price that told him tires were free fuel. The failure was not in his heart. It was in the price.

The fix, when it came, was not to close the kiln or abolish bricks. The district required clean fuel and levied a charge on tire-burning that roughly matched the health damage it caused. Suddenly Pheakdey's "free" fuel carried its true cost. His bricks rose in price — to what they had always really cost, once the lungs were counted. He still sold bricks; he simply sold the right number, made the honest way. Two of his customers went back to the kilns that had been telling the truth all along.

Years later a student from the city interviewed Mrs. Channary for a school report on the environment. She did not use his vocabulary. But she said the thing his textbook was trying to say, and said it better:

"The bricks were never cheap. We were paying for them. They just never sent us the bill."
