# Corporate Social Responsibility — Analogy Bridge
# ការទទួលខុសត្រូវសង្គមសាជីវកម្ម — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

## Analogy 1: Building a House on the Floodplain / ការសង់ផ្ទះលើទីទំនាប

**The Story:**
In rural Cambodia you build a house from the ground up: first the foundation posts driven deep, then the floor platform, then the walls, and finally the decorated roof gable that everyone admires from the road. No one builds the gable first. A beautiful gable on posts that were never driven deep will collapse in the first flood.

Carroll's pyramid of CSR is built the same way. The economic foundation goes first (the deep posts — the business must stand). Then the legal floor. Then the ethical walls. The philanthropy is the carved roof gable — the part visitors admire — but it is the *last* thing built and the *least* load-bearing.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | CSR Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation posts | Economic responsibility | ការទទួលខុសត្រូវសេដ្ឋកិច្ច |
| Floor platform | Legal responsibility | ច្បាប់ |
| Walls | Ethical responsibility | សីលធម៌ |
| Decorated roof gable | Philanthropic responsibility | សប្បុរសធម៌ |
| A gable built first, posts skipped | Greenwashing | ការលាបពណ៌បៃតង |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A house is built once and finished. CSR layers must all be maintained *continuously and at the same time* — you can't "finish" being ethical and move on.

## Analogy 2: The Wedding Host / ម្ចាស់ផ្ទះអាពាហ៍ពិពាហ៍

**The Story:**
A family throws a famous, generous wedding (អាពាហ៍ពិពាហ៍) — a huge tent, a band, food for 800 guests. The whole village talks about their generosity. But suppose this same family had borrowed the money and never repaid the lender, and treated the hired cooks rudely and underpaid them. Is the family truly generous, or just *performing* generosity for the watching village?

A company that runs a lavish charity gala while cheating suppliers and underpaying workers is exactly this wedding host: spectacular at the top layer, hollow underneath.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | CSR Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| The lavish wedding feast | Philanthropic display | ការបង្ហាញសប្បុរសធម៌ |
| Repaying the lender | Economic/legal soundness | ភាពមាំមួនច្បាប់ |
| Treating the hired cooks well | Ethical responsibility | សីលធម៌ |
| Village admiring the tent | Public / PR audience | ទស្សនិកជនសាធារណៈ |
| Borrowed, unpaid generosity | Greenwashing | ការលាបពណ៌បៃតង |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A wedding is a single event; the village eventually forgets. A company's conduct is ongoing and increasingly documented — by ESG ratings, audits, and journalists — so the hollow middle is harder to keep hidden over time.

## Analogy 3: The Doctor's Oath / សម្បថរបស់វេជ្ជបណ្ឌិត

**The Story:**
A doctor must *first* do no harm before doing any good. We would not praise a surgeon who volunteers at free clinics on weekends (a generous deed) if during the week they operate carelessly and injure patients. The duty not to harm ranks below — and before — the credit for extra good deeds.

CSR works identically. The ethical duty not to harm workers, communities, and the environment ranks beneath the philanthropic credit for donations. Do no harm *first*; give back *after*.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | CSR Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| "First, do no harm" | Ethical layer (avoid harm) | កុំធ្វើទុក្ខ |
| Following medical regulations | Legal layer | ច្បាប់ |
| Running a viable practice | Economic layer | សេដ្ឋកិច្ច |
| Weekend free-clinic volunteering | Philanthropic layer | សប្បុរសធម៌ |
| Praising the volunteer despite weekday harm | Mistaking philanthropy for full responsibility | ច្រឡំ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A doctor serves one clear master — the patient. A company serves many stakeholders with conflicting interests, so "do no harm" is harder to define when helping one group can hurt another.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three teach the same order of operations: **the visible, admired good deed sits at the top — and it is worth nothing if the load-bearing layers beneath it are hollow.** The house needs its posts before its gable, the host must pay before they feast, and the doctor must do no harm before doing extra good. Philanthropy is the gable, the feast, the weekend clinic. It is never a substitute for the foundation of profitable, lawful, ethical conduct.
