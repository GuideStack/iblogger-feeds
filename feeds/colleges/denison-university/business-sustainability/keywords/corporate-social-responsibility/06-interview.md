# Corporate Social Responsibility — Journalist Interview
# ការទទួលខុសត្រូវសង្គមសាជីវកម្ម — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sothea Pich, a business ethics scholar at the Royal University of Phnom Penh who advises companies on responsibility programs.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sothea, "corporate social responsibility" is one of those phrases everyone uses and few define. What does it actually mean?

**Dr. Sothea Pich:** At its core, CSR is the idea that a business owes society more than just a product and a profit. Society grants companies real privileges — limited liability, legal protection, use of public infrastructure and labor. CSR says: in return, the company carries obligations. The most useful way to organize those obligations is Archie Carroll's 1991 pyramid.

**Ratana:** Walk me through the pyramid.

**Dr. Sothea:** Four layers, bottom to top. First, *economic* — be profitable, because a failed business helps no one. Second, *legal* — make that profit within the law. Third, *ethical* — do what is right even where the law is silent, such as not harming a community whose protection isn't yet written into Cambodian regulation. Fourth, at the top, *philanthropic* — give back, sponsor a school, fund a clinic. The key insight is the order: philanthropy is the smallest and most voluntary layer, sitting on top of three more fundamental ones.

**Ratana:** Most companies seem to spend their CSR energy on that top layer — donations, sponsorships.

**Dr. Sothea:** Exactly, and that is the central problem in practice. Philanthropy is visible, photogenic, and easy. You write a check, you hang a banner. The ethical layer beneath it — paying fair wages, controlling pollution, treating suppliers honestly — is invisible and hard. So companies over-invest in the apex and under-invest in the middle. When the result is a beautiful charity program sitting on top of poor labor or environmental practices, we call it greenwashing.

**Ratana:** Is the Friedman view — that a company's only job is profit — simply wrong, then?

**Dr. Sothea:** Not wrong, but incomplete. Milton Friedman said the social responsibility of business is to increase profits within the rules of the game. Carroll doesn't throw that away — he makes it the *bottom two layers* of the pyramid. The economic and legal duties are exactly Friedman's view. Carroll simply argues there are two more layers above: ethical and philanthropic. So it's less a contradiction than an expansion.

**Ratana:** People also throw around "ESG" and "shared value." How do those relate to CSR?

**Dr. Sothea:** Think of them as three stages of the same conversation. CSR is the original moral framing — the company's duty. ESG — Environmental, Social, Governance — is the *measurement* layer that investors and regulators added; it turns fuzzy responsibility into scored, disclosed data so capital markets can compare firms. Shared value, from Porter and Kramer, is the most strategic version: instead of profiting and then donating, you design the business so that profit and social benefit are the same act — for instance, a company that profits precisely by employing rural workers fairly. CSR was an add-on; ESG made it measurable; shared value makes it the business model.

**Ratana:** Give me a Cambodian example of CSR done well versus done badly.

**Dr. Sothea:** Done badly: a factory that violates working-hour limits and discharges untreated wastewater, but funds a high-profile scholarship program and publicizes it heavily. The apex is glowing; the middle is hollow. Done well: a social enterprise that builds the social good into its core operations — employing and training rural artisans at fair wages to produce goods for export. There is no separate charity to point to, because the responsible conduct *is* the business. That second model is much harder to fake.

**Ratana:** How can an ordinary observer — a journalist, a consumer — tell genuine CSR from greenwashing?

**Dr. Sothea:** Ignore the donations for a moment and look at the layers underneath. Are workers paid and treated lawfully and fairly? Is there a real, working channel for community complaints, with responses you can verify? Are environmental impacts measured and disclosed, ideally to a recognized standard? If those lower layers are solid, the philanthropy on top is icing on a real cake. If the lower layers are rotten, the philanthropy is just decoration on an empty box.

**Ratana:** Final question. For Cambodia's economy specifically, why does this matter now?

**Dr. Sothea:** Because Cambodia's biggest markets and lenders are adopting ESG disclosure rules. Foreign buyers and banks increasingly look past the charity banner and audit the ethical layer directly. The companies that have quietly built solid foundations — fair labor, clean operations — will keep their contracts. The ones relying on photogenic philanthropy to cover weak practices will be exposed. So CSR, properly understood as the full pyramid and not just the top, is becoming a condition of doing business, not a nice gesture.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sothea.
