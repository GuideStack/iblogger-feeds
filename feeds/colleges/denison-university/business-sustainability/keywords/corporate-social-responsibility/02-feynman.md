# Corporate Social Responsibility — Feynman Explanation
# ការទទួលខុសត្រូវសង្គមសាជីវកម្ម — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Think about a popular noodle restaurant on your street in Phnom Penh. What does it *owe*, and to whom?

Let's build the answer one floor at a time, like stacking a layer cake (នំជាន់).

**Floor 1 — Make money.** This sounds selfish, but it's the foundation. If the restaurant loses money and closes, it pays no salaries, buys from no farmers, feeds no one. A business that can't survive can't do any good for anybody. So the first responsibility is simply: *be a viable business.*

**Floor 2 — Obey the law.** The restaurant must have its license, pay its taxes, follow food-safety rules. The law is society's list of "the minimum you must not break." Making money is only allowed *inside* the law.

**Floor 3 — Be ethical, even where there's no law.** Here it gets interesting. Suppose there's no law forcing the restaurant to give its dishwasher a day off, or to not dump used oil in the canal at night. The law is silent. But you'd still feel something is *wrong* if they overworked the dishwasher or poisoned the canal. That feeling is the ethical floor: doing right even when no rule forces you to.

**Floor 4 — Give back.** On top of everything, the restaurant might sponsor the local pagoda festival, or give leftover food to monks each morning. Nobody requires this. It's a gift. This is the "good citizen" floor.

---

Now stack them up. Floor 1 at the bottom, Floor 4 at the top. That stack is called **Carroll's Pyramid of Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)** — the responsibilities a business has to society, in order.

- Economic (make money) — **required by the market**
- Legal (obey the law) — **required by society**
- Ethical (do right anyway) — **expected by society**
- Philanthropic (give back) — **nice, but voluntary**

---

**Here is the trap people fall into.**

Imagine the restaurant dumps oil in the canal at night (breaking the ethical floor) — but every Pchum Ben it hands out rice to the poor and takes lots of photos for Facebook (the giving floor). They are loudly doing Floor 4 while quietly failing Floor 3.

That's called **greenwashing**: spending more effort *looking* responsible than *being* responsible. The gift on top is real, but it's covering a hole in the middle.

The pyramid protects you from this trick. You can't skip the bottom floors and jump to the top. Giving away rice does not cancel out poisoning the canal. A responsible business builds from the bottom: make money honestly, obey the law, treat people right — *then* give back.

---

**One more thing you'll hear: CSR, ESG, and "shared value."** They're cousins.

- **CSR** is the whole idea above — your duty to society. Often it's a side program: "the company has a charity foundation."
- **ESG** is how investors *measure* it — they give a company a score for Environment, Social, and Governance, so they can decide where to put money.
- **Shared value** is the smartest version: instead of making money first and giving back later, you make money *by* solving a problem — like a company that profits by employing rural weavers at fair wages. The good and the profit are the same activity.

---

**The simple version:**

A business owes society four things, stacked like a cake: survive, obey, do right, and give back — in that order. And no amount of generous giving on top can paper over breaking the law or treating people badly underneath.
