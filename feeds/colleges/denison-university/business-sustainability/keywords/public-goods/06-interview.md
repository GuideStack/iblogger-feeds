# Public Goods — Journalist Interview
# ទំនិញសាធារណៈ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Maly Hour, public-finance and environmental economist at the Royal University of Law and Economics.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Maly, in everyday speech "public good" just means something good for the public. Economists mean something more precise. What is it?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Exactly — and the confusion matters. In economics a public good is defined by two technical properties, not by being benevolent. First, non-excludability: you cannot prevent non-payers from enjoying it. Second, non-rivalry: one person's enjoyment does not reduce what is left for others. Clean air has both. So does national defense, a lighthouse, a radio broadcast. A hospital, by contrast, is good for the public but is *not* a public good in this sense — you can exclude non-payers, and a bed used by one patient is unavailable to another. The distinction is the whole point, because it predicts which things markets can supply and which they cannot.

**Ratana:** Why can't markets supply the true public goods?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Because a business survives by charging, and charging requires the ability to withhold the product from those who don't pay. If you cannot exclude non-payers, you cannot charge, and if you cannot charge, you cannot profit. On top of that, every potential beneficiary realizes they will enjoy the good whether they contribute or not, so each has a private incentive to free-ride. When everyone free-rides, contributions collapse and the good is underprovided — frequently to zero. The market is not failing through malice or inefficiency; it simply has no grip on something it cannot sell.

**Ratana:** Give me a Cambodian example where this is playing out.

**Dr. Maly Hour:** The clearest is the health of the Tonle Sap and the forests that feed it. A flourishing Tonle Sap regulates floods, replenishes fish stocks, and supports the food security of millions — benefits no one can be excluded from and that one person enjoying does not deny another. That is a textbook public good. No private company will pay to protect it, because it cannot bill the millions who benefit. So protection has to come from public budgets, protected-area designation, and increasingly from payment-for-ecosystem-services arrangements where outside funders pay communities to conserve. Those schemes are deliberate attempts to build a market where nature left none.

**Ratana:** You mentioned forests. Is a forest's carbon storage a public good too?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Yes, and a global one. When Prey Lang stores carbon, it stabilizes the climate for the entire planet — utterly non-excludable, utterly non-rival. This is why standing forests are chronically undervalued by markets: their greatest service, climate regulation, is a public good with no buyer. Carbon credit markets are an ingenious if imperfect attempt to manufacture a buyer — to let someone pay for the public good of avoided emissions. When they work, they convert an unsellable benefit into a revenue stream that makes conservation pay. When they are poorly designed, they produce credits for protection that would have happened anyway.

**Ratana:** Climate is often called the ultimate public good problem. Why is it so much harder than a village dyke?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Scale and strangers. A village can solve its public-good problem because neighbors know each other, can apply social pressure, and ultimately can be compelled by a local authority everyone recognizes. Climate stability is shared by eight billion people across nearly two hundred sovereign states, with no global authority that can compel contribution. Every country is tempted to free-ride on others' emission cuts while enjoying the shared stable climate. The good is maximally non-excludable and the "neighbors" cannot be made to pay. That is precisely why climate treaties are so fragile — they are an attempt to solve the largest free-rider problem in human history without a global enforcer.

**Ratana:** So is the answer always government?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Not always government, but always *some* collective institution that overrides individual free-riding. Sometimes it is the state funding the good through taxes. Sometimes it is a binding international agreement. Sometimes it is a community institution governed by shared norms, as Elinor Ostrom famously documented for commons management. And sometimes clever design partially privatizes the good — toll roads, carbon markets, GPS. The common thread is never "trust voluntary goodwill." Goodwill is exactly what the free-rider problem erodes. You need a rule that closes the escape route.

**Ratana:** Final question. What should a business student take from this?

**Dr. Maly Hour:** Two things. First, recognize that many of the resources your business depends on — clean water, a stable climate, a healthy ecosystem — are public goods that no one is paid to maintain, which means they are perpetually at risk and perpetually undervalued until they fail. Second, understand that the frontier of sustainable business is precisely about inventing ways to *get paid* for protecting public goods — ecosystem-service payments, green bonds, carbon markets. The entrepreneur who can build a credible bridge between a public good and a paying beneficiary is solving one of the deepest problems in economics.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Maly.
