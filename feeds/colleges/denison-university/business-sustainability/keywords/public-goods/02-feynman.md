# Public Goods — Feynman Explanation
# ទំនិញសាធារណៈ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

Think about a bowl of noodles. If I eat it, you cannot. And if you do not pay the cook, you do not get a bowl. The cook can refuse you, and your eating uses the noodles up. That is an ordinary thing to buy and sell — economists call it a **private good**. Markets handle these beautifully.

Now think about something completely different: the light from a streetlamp at the corner of your soi.

Ask two questions about it.

**First: if you don't pay for it, can anyone stop you from using its light?** No. The light just falls on the street. Anyone walking past uses it, payer or not. We say it is **non-excludable** (មិនអាចបដិសេធ) — you cannot shut anyone out.

**Second: when you walk under the lamp, is there less light left for the next person?** No. Your using the light takes nothing away from anyone else. We say it is **non-rival** (មិនប្រកួត) — one person's use does not use it up.

A thing with *both* of those properties — cannot exclude anyone, and one person's use does not subtract from another's — is a **public good** (ទំនិញសាធារណៈ). Clean air is one. National defense is one. A lighthouse, a radio broadcast, a stable climate — all public goods.

---

Here is why markets struggle with them. Let me tell it as a small problem.

Your soi is dark and dangerous. Everyone wants a streetlamp. So a neighbor comes around collecting money to buy one.

You think: "If everyone *else* pays, the lamp gets built, and the light will fall on me anyway — I can't be excluded. So why should I pay? I'll save my money and enjoy the light for free."

The clever thing is — *everyone* thinks exactly that. Everyone reasons, "let the others pay." So almost no one contributes, and the lamp never gets bought. Not because people don't want it — they all want it! — but because each person is better off letting someone else foot the bill.

This is the famous **free-rider problem** (បញ្ហាជិះឥតបង់ថ្លៃ). When you cannot exclude non-payers, everyone wants to ride for free, and the thing everyone wants ends up provided by no one.

---

Notice the strange result. For private goods, the market makes the right amount on its own. For public goods, the market makes *too little* — often nothing — even when everybody would be happier paying their share. The market is not lazy or stupid here; it simply has no hook to grab onto, because you can't sell what you can't withhold.

So how do public goods ever get made? Usually by everyone agreeing — *in advance, together* — to be forced to pay. That is, in plain terms, what taxes are. The commune collects a little from everyone and buys the streetlamp for all. Nobody can free-ride because nobody had a choice. We trade away the freedom to dodge the bill in exchange for actually getting the lamp.

---

Now make it bigger. Clean air over Phnom Penh. A protected forest like Prey Lang. A stable climate for the whole planet. These are the biggest public goods there are — and they have the biggest free-rider problem, because no single person or even single country can be charged for them or excluded from them. That is exactly why protecting them needs governments, treaties, and shared rules. A market, left alone, will never produce enough clean air, because nobody can sell you a breath and stop your neighbor from taking one too.

---

**The simple version:**

A public good is something you can't be shut out of, and that you don't use up by enjoying. Because nobody can be charged, everybody waits for everybody else to pay — and so it never gets made by the market. The fix is to decide together to pay together. That is what taxes are for, and it is why clean air and a safe climate are jobs no market can do alone.
