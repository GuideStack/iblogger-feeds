# Public Goods — Analogy Bridge
# ទំនិញសាធារណៈ — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-31*

---

## Analogy 1: The Fireworks Over the River / កាំជ្រួចលើទន្លេ

**The Story:**
During the Water Festival, fireworks burst over the Tonle Sap river in Phnom Penh. Whoever paid for them cannot stop the families on the far bank, the tuk-tuk drivers, or the children on rooftops from watching — the sky belongs to everyone who looks up. And one family enjoying the display takes nothing away from another's view. Now imagine trying to *sell* tickets to the fireworks. Anyone could simply step outside and watch for free. Who would buy a ticket? Almost no one — so a purely private fireworks seller would never light them. They get funded by the city, or a sponsor, on behalf of all.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Public Good Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Anyone can watch the sky | Non-excludable | មិនអាចបដិសេធ |
| Your view doesn't dim mine | Non-rival | មិនប្រកួត |
| "Why buy a ticket?" | Free-rider problem | បញ្ហាជិះឥតបង់ថ្លៃ |
| City or sponsor pays | Public / collective provision | ការផ្គត់ផ្គង់សាធារណៈ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Fireworks last minutes and are cheap relative to a city budget. Climate stability or biodiversity costs enormous sums sustained over generations, where free-riding is far more tempting.

## Analogy 2: The Pagoda Bell / កណ្ដឹងវត្ត

**The Story:**
A village pagoda's bell calls the hour and marks ceremonies. Every household hears it; none can be shut out of the sound, and one family hearing it does not make it quieter for the next. If the temple committee asked each family to pay "per ring," every family would shrug — "I'll hear it anyway." So the bell is never funded that way. Instead it is paid for by communal donation, social obligation, and merit-making — a non-market institution that solves the free-rider problem through shared values rather than prices.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Public Good Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Sound reaches all households | Non-excludable | មិនអាចបដិសេធ |
| One family hearing ≠ less for others | Non-rival | មិនប្រកួត |
| "I'll hear it anyway" | Free-riding | ការជិះឥតបង់ថ្លៃ |
| Communal donation / merit | Collective provision via norms | ផ្គត់ផ្គង់រួមតាមតម្លៃសង្គម |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Religious and social norms can fund a village bell among neighbors who know each other. They cannot fund a global public good shared by billions of strangers who will never meet.

## Analogy 3: The Lighthouse and the Open Sea / ភ្លើងបង្ហាញផ្លូវ និងសមុទ្រ

**The Story:**
A lighthouse warns every ship of the rocks. The keeper cannot single out which ships paid and dim the beam for the rest; the light shines for all, and one captain seeing it does not darken it for another. The classic economic question: would a private owner build it? Hardly — passing ships benefit whether they pay or not, so they won't. The lighthouse, the textbook's oldest example of a public good, must be funded collectively by a port authority for the benefit of all who sail.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Public Good Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Beam reaches every ship | Non-excludable | មិនអាចបដិសេធ |
| One ship's safety ≠ less for others | Non-rival | មិនប្រកួត |
| Ships benefit without paying | Free-rider problem | បញ្ហាជិះឥតបង់ថ្លៃ |
| Port authority funds it | Public provision | ការផ្គត់ផ្គង់សាធារណៈ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Modern GPS made navigation partly excludable, showing that technology can sometimes convert a public good into a club good — a reminder that the categories depend on what is technologically possible, not on nature alone.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

Fireworks, a pagoda bell, a lighthouse — each reaches everyone, each is used up by no one, and each defeats the question "who would pay?" That is the signature of a **public good**: enormously valuable, impossible to sell, and therefore reliant on collective provision. The same logic, scaled to the atmosphere and the forests, explains why clean air, biodiversity, and a stable climate cannot be left to the market alone.
