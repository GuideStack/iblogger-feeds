## The Dyke That Everyone Needed and No One Built / ទំនប់ដែលគ្រប់គ្នាត្រូវការ តែគ្មាននរណាសង់

The village of Trapeang Thom sat in a low bend of the floodplain, and every few years the wet-season water rose too high and ruined the rice before harvest. The old people remembered the flood of their youth and the flood of their parents' youth. Everyone agreed on the solution. A long earthen dyke along the eastern edge would hold the worst of the water back and save the fields of perhaps sixty families.

Everyone agreed it should be built. For eleven years, nobody built it.

This is the part of the story worth slowing down for, because it is not a story about laziness or stupidity. The people of Trapeang Thom were neither. It is a story about a particular kind of trap.

The dyke, once built, would protect the whole low bend. You could not build a dyke that protected only the families who paid for it and let the water through onto the families who did not — the water does not read a contribution list. And one family being protected did not use up the protection; the same wall sheltered the next family's field just as well. The dyke, in other words, could not be sold. It could not be withheld from anyone in the bend, and one household's safety took nothing from another's.

So when Old Vouch went around with his proposal — each family to give five days of labor or the cash equivalent — he met the same answer in many forms.

"My field is on the higher side; let the lower families build it."

"If the dyke goes up, my rice is saved whether I dig or not. Why should I be the one to dig?"

"Let me see who else commits first."

None of them were villains. Each was making a perfectly sensible private calculation: *the wall will protect me regardless of whether I help, so the smart move is to let others do the work and enjoy the protection for free.* The trouble was that nearly everyone reasoned this way. And when nearly everyone waits for everyone else, the digging never starts. Year after year the water came, the rice drowned, and the families that had each, individually, made the "smart" choice were, collectively, ruined.

Old Vouch, who had no word for it, had stumbled into what economists call the **free-rider problem**, and he had learned the bitter lesson at its core: a thing that everyone wants will not get made, if everyone can enjoy it without paying for it. The dyke was a **public good** — and public goods do not build themselves out of goodwill, because goodwill is exactly what the free-rider quietly spends on himself.

What finally broke the deadlock was not a change of heart. It was a change of *rule*. A new commune council, prodded by the worst flood in a decade, did something Old Vouch's polite collection box could never do: it made the contribution compulsory. Every household in the protected bend was assessed a share — by labor or by a small levy collected with the land record — and the share was not optional. The free-rider's escape route was closed. No one could enjoy the dyke while dodging the cost, because the cost now arrived whether you volunteered or not.

The grumbling was loud. It was also brief. The dyke went up in a single dry season, sixty families digging because all sixty knew the other fifty-nine were digging too. The next wet season the water rose, pressed against the new wall, and the rice stood.

A young woman from the village, years later, studied economics in Phnom Penh and recognized her own childhood in a single page of her textbook: clean air, national defense, a lighthouse, a flood dyke — goods no one can be shut out of and no one uses up, which the market will never supply and which only a collective decision can. She wrote in the margin a sentence her grandfather might have said: *The dyke was not built when we each loved it. It was built when we each agreed to be made to pay for it.*

That, she understood, is the whole reason taxes exist — and the whole reason that clean air, protected forests, and a stable climate, the great public goods of her generation, will be saved by shared rules or not at all.
