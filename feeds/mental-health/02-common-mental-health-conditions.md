## How to Use This (and How Not To)

**Do** use this to:
- Put a name and a fairer understanding to what you or someone else may be experiencing.
- Recognize when "just stressed" might be something worth getting support for.
- Replace judgment ("they're being dramatic / lazy") with empathy.

**Don't** use this to:
- **Self-diagnose or diagnose others.** Many conditions share symptoms (e.g. trouble concentrating appears in depression, anxiety, ADHD, and PTSD). Naming is a doctor's job.
- **Label people.** A person *has* a condition; they are not the condition.

> A few honest framings used throughout: these conditions are **common**, **treatable**, and **not a personal weakness or a character flaw.** Asking for help is a strength, not a failure.

<a id="02"></a>
## The Conditions at a Glance

| Condition | In one line | A few common signs |
|:----------|:------------|:-------------------|
| **PTSD** | Lasting distress after trauma | Flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, avoidance |
| **Anxiety (GAD)** | Excessive, hard-to-control worry | Restlessness, racing thoughts, tension, dread |
| **Panic disorder** | Sudden surges of intense fear | Racing heart, can't breathe, "I'm dying" feeling |
| **Depression** | Persistent low mood & loss of interest | Hopelessness, fatigue, no joy, sleep/appetite change |
| **Burnout** | Chronic work stress, depleted | Exhaustion, cynicism, reduced performance |
| **OCD** | Intrusive thoughts + compulsions | Unwanted thoughts, rituals to relieve anxiety |
| **Bipolar** | Swings between highs and lows | Manic energy then depressive crashes |
| **ADHD** | Attention/impulse regulation | Distractibility, restlessness, time blindness |
| **Impostor syndrome** | Feeling like a fraud despite success | Self-doubt, fear of being "found out" |

> This is a *starting* vocabulary, not a complete clinical list. Each section below adds signs, what it feels like, and what helps.

<a id="03"></a>
## PTSD — Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

> A condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a **traumatic event** — violence, an accident, abuse, disaster, combat, sudden loss. The danger has passed, but the mind and body stay on alert.

**Common signs**
- **Re-experiencing:** flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories that feel like reliving it.
- **Avoidance:** steering clear of places, people, or topics that are reminders.
- **Hyperarousal:** always "on guard," easily startled, trouble sleeping, irritability.
- **Negative shifts:** numbness, detachment, guilt, loss of interest, a bleak view of the world.

**What it can feel like:** the past intruding on the present — your body reacting to danger that isn't here anymore.

**What helps:** trauma-focused therapy (e.g. CBT, EMDR), sometimes medication, grounding techniques, and a safe support system. **PTSD is treatable** — many people recover well with the right help. (Related but milder/short-term reactions are sometimes called *acute stress*.)

<a id="04"></a>
## Anxiety Disorders (incl. GAD)

> Anxiety is a normal response to threat. It becomes a **disorder** when it's excessive, persistent, and gets in the way of daily life. Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is worry that's hard to switch off, about many things, most days.

**Common signs**
- Excessive, uncontrollable worry; expecting the worst.
- Restlessness, feeling "on edge," difficulty concentrating.
- Physical: muscle tension, fatigue, racing heart, stomach issues, trouble sleeping.

**What it can feel like:** a mind that won't stop scanning for problems, even when things are fine.

**What helps:** therapy (CBT is well-evidenced), relaxation and breathing skills, regular exercise and sleep, limiting caffeine/alcohol, and sometimes medication. *(Other forms include social anxiety and specific phobias.)*

<a id="05"></a>
## Panic Disorder & Panic Attacks

> A **panic attack** is a sudden, intense surge of fear that peaks within minutes — often with no obvious trigger. **Panic disorder** is when attacks recur and you start fearing the next one.

**Common signs (during an attack)**
- Pounding heart, chest tightness, shortness of breath.
- Dizziness, trembling, sweating, tingling.
- A feeling of unreality or that something catastrophic is happening ("I'm dying / losing control").

**What it can feel like:** a false alarm in the body's fear system — terrifying, but **not physically dangerous** and it passes.

**What helps:** learning the attacks are time-limited and survivable, slow breathing, grounding (name 5 things you can see/hear/touch), therapy, and sometimes medication. *(New or unexplained chest symptoms should still be checked by a doctor to rule out physical causes.)*

<a id="06"></a>
## Depression

> More than sadness or a bad week — depression is a **persistent** low mood and/or loss of interest lasting two weeks or more, heavy enough to affect daily life.

**Common signs**
- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness.
- Loss of interest or pleasure in things you used to enjoy (*anhedonia*).
- Fatigue, changes in sleep and appetite, trouble concentrating.
- Feelings of worthlessness or guilt; in serious cases, thoughts of death or self-harm.

**What it can feel like:** a heavy fog — not "lazy" or "ungrateful," but an illness that drains energy and color from life.

**What helps:** therapy, medication, exercise, routine, connection, and sunlight — usually a combination. **Depression is very treatable.** ➡️ If there are **thoughts of self-harm or suicide, treat it as urgent** — see [Getting Help](#13).

<a id="07"></a>
## Burnout

> Not a medical diagnosis in the same sense, but a recognized state of **chronic, unmanaged work (or caregiving) stress** — depletion that built up over time. (The repo has a dedicated piece: [Five Signs of Burnout](./01-five-signs-of-burnout.md).)

**Common signs**
- **Exhaustion** that rest doesn't fix.
- **Cynicism / detachment** from work you once cared about.
- **Reduced effectiveness** — things that were easy now feel impossible.

**What it can feel like:** running on empty with the engine still revving.

**What helps:** real recovery (not just a weekend), boundaries, reducing the chronic stressor at its source, and support. Burnout overlaps with depression — if it persists, get a professional assessment.

---

<a id="08"></a>
## OCD — Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

> A cycle of **obsessions** (unwanted, intrusive thoughts, images, or urges that cause anxiety) and **compulsions** (repeated behaviors or mental acts done to relieve that anxiety). It's far more than "liking things tidy."

**Common signs**
- Intrusive thoughts (contamination, harm, symmetry, "what if I…").
- Rituals: checking, washing, counting, repeating, seeking reassurance.
- Knowing the rituals are excessive but feeling unable to stop.

**What it can feel like:** a brain stuck in a loop, where the ritual brings brief relief that never lasts.

**What helps:** a specific therapy called **ERP** (Exposure and Response Prevention), often with medication. Highly treatable with the right approach.

---

<a id="09"></a>
## Bipolar Disorder

> A condition marked by **shifts in mood, energy, and activity** — between depressive lows and elevated "manic" or "hypomanic" highs.

**Common signs**
- **Manic/hypomanic:** unusually high energy, less need for sleep, racing thoughts, impulsivity, grandiosity.
- **Depressive:** the low-mood signs described under [Depression](#06).
- Cycles that can last days to weeks and disrupt life and relationships.

**What it can feel like:** a mood "thermostat" that swings far beyond ordinary ups and downs.

**What helps:** ongoing professional care — mood-stabilizing medication and therapy, with a steady routine (especially sleep). Diagnosis really matters here, because the highs can feel good and go unrecognized.

---

<a id="10"></a>
## ADHD — Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

> A neurodevelopmental difference in how attention, impulse, and activity are regulated. Often lifelong, frequently undiagnosed in adults — and not a matter of "trying harder."

**Common signs**
- **Inattentive:** distractibility, forgetfulness, losing things, trouble finishing tasks, "time blindness."
- **Hyperactive/impulsive:** restlessness, interrupting, acting before thinking.
- Can co-exist with anxiety and depression.

**What it can feel like:** an interest-driven brain — capable of deep "hyperfocus" yet unable to start the boring-but-important thing.

**What helps:** diagnosis and a mix of strategies — structure, external systems (lists, timers, reminders), coaching/therapy, and sometimes medication. Many people thrive once they understand how their brain works. *(Note: ADHD is a difference, not solely a "disorder" — strengths come with it too.)*

---

<a id="11"></a>
## Impostor Syndrome

> Not a clinical diagnosis, but a very common **experience** — especially in tech: persistent self-doubt and a feeling of being a "fraud" who'll be "found out," despite real competence and evidence of success.

**Common signs**
- Attributing success to luck or timing, not your ability.
- Fear of being exposed as not good enough.
- Over-working to "prove" yourself, or avoiding challenges to avoid "failing."

**What it can feel like:** everyone else has it figured out and you're the exception (they often feel the same).

**What helps:** naming it, talking about it (it's nearly universal in tech), keeping a record of real wins/feedback, mentorship, and separating *feelings* from *facts*. It commonly travels with anxiety — address that if it's heavy.

<a id="12"></a>
## How to Support Someone

> You don't need to be a therapist to help. You need to be a safe, steady human.

**Do**
- **Listen without fixing.** "That sounds really hard — I'm here" beats advice.
- **Take it seriously** and believe them; don't minimize ("just think positive").
- **Ask directly** if you're worried about safety — *asking about suicide does not plant the idea*; it opens a door.
- **Help them reach professional help**, and check back in later.
- **Respect privacy** — support, don't broadcast.

**Don't**
- Don't diagnose, label, or compare ("everyone gets stressed").
- Don't take symptoms personally (irritability/withdrawal are often the illness talking).
- Don't make yourself their only support — encourage professional help and protect your own limits too.

> 🤝 For teams & leads: this connects to building [psychological safety](../procedures/scrum-master/05-coaching-and-team-health.md) and humane [1-on-1s](../procedures/engineering-manager/03-one-on-ones-and-feedback.md) — a culture where people can say "I'm not okay" without fear.

<a id="13"></a>
## Getting Help

> **If you or someone else is in immediate danger or thinking about suicide, contact emergency services or a crisis line right now.** You don't have to be sure it's "serious enough" to reach out.

**Crisis resources**
- **Crisis Text Line** — text **HOME** to **741741**
- **SAMHSA National Helpline (US)** — **1-800-662-4357** (free, confidential, 24/7)
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)** — call or text **988**
- **Your local emergency number** and local mental-health services
- Outside the US: search "**suicide helpline [your country]**" or see the [International Association for Suicide Prevention directory](https://www.iasp.info/resources/Crisis_Centres/)

**Everyday help**
- A doctor / GP is a good first step — they can assess and refer.
- A licensed therapist, counselor, or psychiatrist.
- Employee Assistance Programs (EAP) if your workplace offers one.

> A closing reminder: these conditions are **common and treatable**, getting help is a sign of strength, and **this document is not a substitute for professional care.**
